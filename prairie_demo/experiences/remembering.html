Remembering & Resilience

The History

For over a century, the Canadian government, in partnership with churches, operated residential schools with the explicit goal of eliminating Indigenous cultures, languages, and identities. The Métis, along with First Nations and Inuit children, were taken from their families and communities.

Children were forbidden from speaking their languages—including Michif—and were punished for practicing their cultural traditions. They were separated from their families, often for years at a time, breaking the bonds that carry culture from one generation to the next.

1870s-1880s
First residential schools established in western Canada. Métis children begin to be taken.
1920
Attendance becomes compulsory. Parents face imprisonment for keeping children home.
1960s
The "Sixties Scoop" sees thousands of Indigenous children placed in non-Indigenous foster homes.
1996
The last federally-run residential school in Canada closes.
2008
Prime Minister Stephen Harper delivers formal apology to former students.
2015
Truth and Reconciliation Commission releases its final report with 94 Calls to Action.
2021
Unmarked graves discovered at former residential school sites across Canada.

What Was Lost

The residential school system was designed to sever the ties between children and their cultures. For Métis communities, this meant:

Language: Children were punished for speaking Michif, the unique language that blended their Cree and French heritage. Many survivors never spoke it again, and today Michif is endangered.

Traditional Knowledge: The skills passed from grandparents to grandchildren—beadwork, medicine, hunting, storytelling—were interrupted. Generations lost access to teachings that had flowed for centuries.

Family Bonds: Children grew up without parents, and parents without children. The trauma of separation echoes through generations.

"They tried to take our language, our culture, our identity. But they couldn't take our spirit. We are still here, and we are bringing back what they tried to destroy."
— Métis Elder

Rising Strong: Cultural Revitalization

Despite generations of systematic attempts to erase their culture, the Métis people have shown remarkable resilience. Today, there is a powerful movement to reclaim and revitalize what was nearly lost.

Language Revival: Michif language programs are growing. Elders work with young people to ensure the language survives and thrives. Apps, courses, and immersion programs are bringing Michif to new generations.

Cultural Celebration: Métis Days, jigging competitions, and fiddle festivals bring communities together in celebration. Young people are learning beadwork, sash weaving, and traditional crafts.

Political Recognition: The Métis Nation continues to fight for and win recognition of their rights, land claims, and self-governance.

This prairie experience you're exploring is part of that revitalization—using new technology to share old wisdom, ensuring these teachings continue for generations to come.

Moving Forward Together

Reconciliation is not a destination but a journey. For educators and school leaders, understanding this history is essential to creating spaces where Métis students feel seen, valued, and proud of their heritage.
